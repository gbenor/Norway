# Chapter 3 - From Oslo to Hemsedal: Entering Mountain Norway

Leaving Oslo marked the real beginning of the road trip.

Until this point we had experienced Norway as a city. Now the landscape began to open. Urban streets gave way to forests, lakes, valleys and mountain roads. The drive toward Hemsedal gradually introduced us to a different Norway: quieter, wider and more rural.

This transition was important. The fjords would come later, but first we had to cross inland Norway, where villages sit beside lakes and mountain culture feels close.

## Valdres Region

Valdres is a traditional district in central southern Norway, known for valleys, lakes, farming, mountain cabins and folk traditions. Driving through it gave us an early sense of rural Norwegian life.

The landscape felt peaceful rather than dramatic. There were wooden houses, farms, lakes and long stretches of green countryside. Everything seemed clean and well maintained, even in small places far from major cities.

This became one of our repeated observations during the trip: even remote villages in Norway often feel cared for. Roads are good, signs are clear, public spaces are tidy, and nature is never far away.

## Fagernes and Strandefjorden

Fagernes sits beside Strandefjorden and is one of the main towns of the Valdres region.

For us it was a natural stopping point on the way from Oslo toward Hemsedal. It gave the day a slower rhythm: lake views, a lunch stop, and the feeling that we were no longer in the capital but not yet deep in the fjord landscape.

Strandefjorden was one of the first Norwegian lakes that made us stop and look. The water, the surrounding hills and the calm atmosphere were a preview of what would come later on a larger scale.

## Hemsedal

Hemsedal is often called one of Norway's best ski destinations. In winter it attracts skiers from Norway and abroad. In summer, it becomes a base for hiking, cycling and mountain scenery.

Arriving in Hemsedal felt like entering a mountain village. The slopes, cabins and open spaces made it clear that this is a place built around outdoor life.

What we liked was that the village did not feel like a noisy resort. It felt calm and practical. People come here for the mountains, not for show.

## Evening Near Lykkja

Because June days in Norway are long, evening did not feel like the end of the day. Even late in the evening there was still light, and we could continue exploring.

Near Lykkja, the landscape felt open and peaceful. This was not a famous attraction like Trolltunga or the Flåm Railway. It was simply a beautiful mountain area near where we slept. But these quieter stops became part of the trip's charm.

The long daylight changed our travel rhythm. In many countries, sightseeing ends when the sun sets. In Norway in June, the evening often becomes another opportunity.

## Question Along the Road: Is Norway Really That Rich?

During the early part of the trip, we also talked about Norway's wealth.

Norway is one of the richest countries in the world, but the reasons are more complex than simply "oil". Oil and gas played a huge role, especially after discoveries in the North Sea. But Norway also had strong institutions, a small population, hydropower, maritime expertise and a political culture that saved much of its petroleum income in a sovereign wealth fund rather than spending it immediately.

This helped explain something we kept noticing: good roads, clean public spaces, strong infrastructure and high prices. Norway is expensive, but it also feels like a country where public systems work.

This question would return later when we saw tunnels, ferries, mountain roads and remote infrastructure that must be extremely expensive to build and maintain.
