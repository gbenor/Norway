# Lital & Gilad Explore Norway

## A Journey Through Fjords, Mountains and Norwegian Culture

June 2026

The idea for this trip did not begin as a classic holiday plan. It started because Lital had an EMDR conference in Oslo. Instead of treating the journey as a short professional trip, we decided to take advantage of being in Norway and continue into a full road trip across the country.

At first, the plan sounded simple: arrive in Oslo, see the famous fjords, experience something close to the classic "Norway in a Nutshell" route, and finish in Bergen. But very quickly the trip became much more than a list of attractions.

For more than a week, it felt as if we were living inside a postcard. Every day brought a new combination of water, cliffs, snow, tunnels, ferries, villages, waterfalls and mountains. Sometimes the scenery looked too perfect to be real. A short drive could move us from a quiet city street to a forest, from a forest to a mountain road, from a mountain road to a fjord, and from a fjord into a tunnel that seemed to continue forever.

What stayed with us most was not only the beauty. It was the way Norway works with its geography. Where there are mountains, Norway builds tunnels. Where there are fjords, Norway uses ferries. Where there are waterfalls, Norway generates power. Where there is snow, Norway builds a culture around skiing. Nature in Norway is not just something people admire. It shapes the roads, the towns, the sports, the energy system and the identity of the country.

This manuscript is both a memory and a guide. It tells the story of what we saw and did, but also the questions we asked along the way: Why are the fjords salty? Why is Norway so rich? Why are there so many tunnels? How do you build a trail to Trolltunga? Why does a ferry feel like a road? Why are all the people in Vigeland Park naked?

# Chapter 1 - First Impressions of Oslo

Our Norway journey began in Oslo.

The first surprise was how calm the city felt. We expected a European capital, and of course Oslo is a capital city, but it did not feel crowded or aggressive. Compared with cities such as London, Paris or Rome, Oslo felt quieter, cleaner and more open. The city seemed less obsessed with traffic and more designed around people, water, public spaces and access to nature.

Another surprise was how close nature appeared to be. Even from the city centre, hills and forests were visible in the distance. At the time we did not yet understand how important this connection to nature is in Norwegian life. Over the next week, we would see it again and again: in skiing culture, mountain cabins, hiking trails, ferries, waterfalls, fjords and even the design of the cities.

Oslo became our introduction to Norway not because it was dramatic like the fjords, but because it showed the Norwegian balance: city life, culture, modern design, history and nature all living very close together.

## Exploring Oslo by City Bike

One of the best decisions of our first day was renting Oslo city bikes.

The public bike system made the city feel open and easy to explore. Stations are spread throughout central Oslo, and the bikes can be unlocked using a mobile app. For visitors, this is a very practical way to move between neighbourhoods and attractions without constantly waiting for public transport or paying for taxis.

The cycling infrastructure felt safer and more organised than in many other cities. There were dedicated lanes, calm streets and a general sense that bikes were part of normal city life. We could start near the waterfront, move through the city, stop whenever something looked interesting, continue toward parks and museums, and cover much more ground than we would have managed by walking alone.

For us, the city bikes gave the first day a sense of freedom. They also changed the mood of the city. Oslo did not feel like a place where we had to move from attraction to attraction. It felt like a city we could flow through.

Practical note for future travellers: if the weather is reasonable and you are comfortable cycling, renting city bikes is one of the best ways to see Oslo in a single day.

## Oslo Opera House

The Oslo Opera House was our first major stop.

Unlike traditional opera houses, this building does not ask visitors only to look at it from the outside. It invites people to walk on it. The white sloping roof rises from the waterfront like a sheet of ice or a glacier sliding into the Oslofjord. Visitors can climb from street level all the way to the roof and look back over the harbour, the islands and the city.

The building was designed by the Norwegian architectural firm Snøhetta and opened in 2008. It quickly became one of Oslo's most recognisable modern landmarks, not only because of its appearance but because of the way it turns a cultural building into a public space. You do not need a ticket to experience the most famous part of it. You simply walk up.

What made our visit more memorable was the atmosphere around the city that day. There was a march with orchestras and performers, filling the streets with music and movement. It was not something we had planned, but it gave our first hours in Norway a festive feeling. Instead of arriving into a quiet, formal capital, we arrived into a city with music in the streets and people gathered around the waterfront.

The Opera House was a perfect first stop because it introduced several themes that would repeat throughout the trip: modern design, public access, water, and the Norwegian habit of turning important places into spaces people can actually use.

## Vigeland Sculpture Park

If the Opera House represents modern Norway, Vigeland Park represents something more human and timeless.

Vigeland Park is the largest sculpture park in the world created by a single artist. Norwegian sculptor Gustav Vigeland designed more than 200 sculptures and over 600 human figures for the park. The works explore the full human journey: childhood, family, love, conflict, aging, loneliness and death.

What makes the park unusual is that the sculptures are not about kings, generals or famous historical events. They are about ordinary people and ordinary emotions. Children play. Parents hold their children. Couples argue. Old people sit quietly. Human bodies appear strong, weak, funny, angry and vulnerable.

One question came immediately: why is everyone naked?

At first, the nudity can feel strange, especially in a public park. But it is central to the idea of the place. Vigeland removed clothing, uniforms and social markers so the figures would not represent specific professions, classes, periods or nationalities. Without clothes, the sculptures become more universal. They are not rich or poor, modern or ancient, Norwegian or foreign. They are simply human.

That helped us understand the park differently. It was not trying to be provocative. It was trying to show human life without decoration.

We also noticed how many children appear in the sculptures. This is because the park is built around the cycle of life. Children represent beginnings, energy, dependence, frustration, joy and the continuation of life. The famous Angry Boy statue is a perfect example. It is funny because it is so recognisable. Anyone who has seen a child lose patience understands it immediately.

The most famous work is the Monolith, a 17-metre granite column carved from a single block of stone. It contains 121 intertwined human figures climbing toward the sky. Vigeland never gave one final explanation of its meaning, which may be why it remains so powerful. It can represent struggle, ambition, community, spiritual growth, the cycle of life, or the human desire to rise above ordinary existence.

Walking through the park, we noticed how naturally locals used it. People jogged between the sculptures. Families walked with children. Tourists moved slowly from one group of figures to the next. Art was not isolated behind museum walls. It was part of daily life.

This was one of the first places where we felt that Norway invests in public spaces not only as decoration, but as places where people are meant to spend time.

## Fram Museum

One of the highlights of Oslo was the Fram Museum.

The museum houses the original Fram, one of the most famous exploration ships in Norwegian history. The Fram carried explorers such as Fridtjof Nansen, Otto Sverdrup and Roald Amundsen into the Arctic and Antarctic. Amundsen later became the first person to reach the South Pole.

Walking aboard the ship made Norwegian exploration history tangible. The cabins were small. The living conditions looked harsh. It was difficult to imagine spending months or years trapped in polar ice, surrounded by darkness, cold and uncertainty.

What impressed us most was that the story was not only about adventure. It was about endurance, planning and scientific curiosity. The Fram was built to survive being frozen into ice rather than crushed by it. The ship was designed for one of the most extreme environments on Earth.

The museum helped us understand something important about Norway: this is a country shaped by harsh geography and climate. Exploration, skiing, hiking and survival in nature are not separate from national identity. They are part of it.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Fram Museum and ship interior]

## Akershus Fortress

Akershus Fortress stands above Oslo Harbour and has protected the city for more than seven centuries.

Construction began around the year 1299 under King Haakon V. Over time the fortress served as a military stronghold, royal residence, prison and symbol of Norwegian sovereignty. It survived wars, sieges and occupation, including the Second World War.

For us, Akershus was valuable because it connected Oslo's modern waterfront to its older history. From the fortress walls, the strategic importance of the location becomes clear. Whoever controlled this point controlled the harbour and access to the city.

Unlike some European castles that feel isolated from modern life, Akershus still feels integrated into Oslo. It sits beside the harbour, near modern buildings and public spaces, reminding visitors that the city has layers: medieval, maritime, royal, military and modern.

## Oslo Reflection

By the end of our first Oslo day, we had seen a surprising range of places: a modern opera house, a public sculpture park, a polar exploration ship, a medieval fortress, a music-filled street march and ordinary city life by bike.

The biggest lesson was that Oslo is not a city to understand only through monuments. It is a city to move through: by bike, by foot, by ferry, by metro and eventually by train into the hills.

We arrived expecting Norway's greatest beauty to begin later, in the fjords. Oslo showed us that the Norwegian story begins already in the capital: with water, public space, outdoor life and a deep connection to nature.

# Chapter 2 - Holmenkollen, Nordmarka and the Norwegian Love of Skiing

The next day we decided to explore another side of Oslo.

Instead of staying downtown, we took the T-bane toward Holmenkollen. This became one of our favourite discoveries in Oslo. Few capital cities allow visitors to board a metro in the centre and arrive in mountain scenery less than an hour later.

The journey itself felt like part of the attraction. As the train climbed higher, city streets gradually gave way to hillside neighbourhoods, trees and open views. The Oslofjord appeared below, and the city slowly became smaller.

This was our first real lesson in how close nature is to everyday life in Norway.

## Holmenkollen Ski Jump

Before visiting Norway, skiing was simply another winter sport to us. Holmenkollen showed that in Norway, skiing is much more than sport. It is part of national identity.

The ski jump dominates the hillside above Oslo. Competitions have been held in the Holmenkollen area since the nineteenth century, and the site is one of the most famous winter sports venues in the world. The current structure is modern, dramatic and visible from many parts of the city.

Standing near the jump, one question came naturally: can people actually ski here?

Yes. Holmenkollen is not just a tourist attraction. It remains an active venue for ski jumping and Nordic skiing competitions. The idea that such an important sports site is reachable by ordinary metro from the city centre says a lot about Oslo.

What impressed us most was not only the skiing culture but also the engineering. The structure looks almost impossible from below: a huge, elegant ramp placed high above the city, combining sport, architecture and landscape. It is not hidden away in a remote Olympic park. It stands above Oslo as part of the city's identity.

Looking at it, we had the same reaction that would return again and again in Norway: how did they build this here? The ski jump is a man-made structure, but it feels connected to the hill, the forest and the fjord below. It was one of the first examples we saw of Norwegian engineering placed directly into dramatic natural surroundings.

The view from Holmenkollen is also one of the best in the region. From above, Oslo looks less like a dense capital and more like a city placed carefully between forest and fjord.

## What Is Cross-Country Skiing?

At Holmenkollen we also discussed cross-country skiing.

Unlike downhill skiing, which is focused on descending slopes, cross-country skiing is about travelling across long distances on snow. It combines endurance, rhythm and technique. In Norway it is not only an Olympic sport but a normal part of winter life.

Children learn it early. Families go skiing together. Trails are maintained in forests and mountains. For many Norwegians, skiing is a way to stay connected to nature during the long winter.

This helped explain why Norway, with a relatively small population, is so successful in winter sports. Skiing is not only trained in stadiums. It is embedded in everyday culture.

## Nordmarka Forest

After Holmenkollen we continued toward Nordmarka.

Within minutes, Oslo disappeared. The city gave way to forests, lakes and quiet paths. What surprised us most was not only the beauty of the forest, but how normal it seemed for locals to be there. Families walked along trails. Cyclists moved through the woods. Children played near the lakes.

This was not wilderness reserved for tourists. It was part of everyday life.

One of the clearest lessons of the trip emerged here: Norwegians do not simply visit nature. They live with it.

Nordmarka also changed how we understood Oslo. The city is not just a capital with parks. It is a capital with a huge forest attached to it by public transport. For travellers who have only one extra day in Oslo, Holmenkollen and Nordmarka are among the best ways to understand the city beyond museums and monuments.

## Evening Thoughts

This second Oslo day prepared us for the rest of the trip.

We began with a metro ride and ended with a much better understanding of Norway's outdoor identity. The ski jump, the forest, the views and the quiet trails all pointed toward the same idea: in Norway, nature is not a background. It is a central part of life.

The next day we would leave Oslo by car and begin the road trip west toward mountains, valleys and eventually fjords.

# Chapter 3 - From Oslo to Hemsedal: Entering Mountain Norway

Leaving Oslo marked the real beginning of the road trip.

Until this point we had experienced Norway as a city. Now the landscape began to open. Urban streets gave way to forests, lakes, valleys and mountain roads. The drive toward Hemsedal gradually introduced us to a different Norway: quieter, wider and more rural.

This transition was important. The fjords would come later, but first we had to cross inland Norway, where villages sit beside lakes and mountain culture feels close.

## Valdres Region

Valdres is a traditional district in central southern Norway, known for valleys, lakes, farming, mountain cabins and folk traditions. Driving through it gave us an early sense of rural Norwegian life.

The landscape felt peaceful rather than dramatic. There were wooden houses, farms, lakes and long stretches of green countryside. Everything seemed clean and well maintained, even in small places far from major cities.

This became one of our repeated observations during the trip: even remote villages in Norway often feel cared for. Roads are good, signs are clear, public spaces are tidy, and nature is never far away.

## Fagernes and Strandefjorden

Fagernes sits beside Strandefjorden and is one of the main towns of the Valdres region.

For us it was a natural stopping point on the way from Oslo toward Hemsedal. It gave the day a slower rhythm: lake views, a lunch stop, and the feeling that we were no longer in the capital but not yet deep in the fjord landscape.

Strandefjorden was one of the first Norwegian lakes that made us stop and look. The water, the surrounding hills and the calm atmosphere were a preview of what would come later on a larger scale.

## Hemsedal

Hemsedal is often called one of Norway's best ski destinations. In winter it attracts skiers from Norway and abroad. In summer, it becomes a base for hiking, cycling and mountain scenery.

Arriving in Hemsedal felt like entering a mountain village. The slopes, cabins and open spaces made it clear that this is a place built around outdoor life.

What we liked was that the village did not feel like a noisy resort. It felt calm and practical. People come here for the mountains, not for show.

## Evening Near Lykkja

Because June days in Norway are long, evening did not feel like the end of the day. Even late in the evening there was still light, and we could continue exploring.

Near Lykkja, the landscape felt open and peaceful. This was not a famous attraction like Trolltunga or the Flåm Railway. It was simply a beautiful mountain area near where we slept. But these quieter stops became part of the trip's charm.

The long daylight changed our travel rhythm. In many countries, sightseeing ends when the sun sets. In Norway in June, the evening often becomes another opportunity.

## Question Along the Road: Is Norway Really That Rich?

During the early part of the trip, we also talked about Norway's wealth.

Norway is one of the richest countries in the world, but the reasons are more complex than simply "oil". Oil and gas played a huge role, especially after discoveries in the North Sea. But Norway also had strong institutions, a small population, hydropower, maritime expertise and a political culture that saved much of its petroleum income in a sovereign wealth fund rather than spending it immediately.

This helped explain something we kept noticing: good roads, clean public spaces, strong infrastructure and high prices. Norway is expensive, but it also feels like a country where public systems work.

This question would return later when we saw tunnels, ferries, mountain roads and remote infrastructure that must be extremely expensive to build and maintain.

# Chapter 4 - Into Fjord Country: Borgund, the Snow Road and Undredal

Leaving Hemsedal marked the beginning of the part of the trip we had been anticipating most.

Until now, Norway had been beautiful, but still somewhat familiar. Mountains, forests and lakes exist in many countries. What makes Norway unique are the fjords: deep valleys carved by glaciers and flooded by the sea.

This day would take us from mountain valleys into one of the most spectacular landscapes in Europe.

## Borgund Stave Church

The first major stop of the day was Borgund Stave Church.

From a distance it almost looks like something from a fantasy novel rather than a real church. Dark timber walls rise into multiple layers of steep roofs decorated with dragon heads. The building feels more Viking than Christian.

That impression is not entirely wrong.

Borgund was built around the year 1180, during the period when Norway was transitioning from Norse traditions to Christianity. Many older cultural symbols survived this transition. The dragon heads on the roof are one example. They are connected to older Nordic beliefs and were believed to protect the building from evil spirits.

One of the questions we asked while walking around the church was simple: how can a wooden building survive for more than 800 years?

The answer lies partly in craftsmanship and partly in maintenance. The builders used large pine timbers and a stave construction method based on vertical wooden posts. The steep roofs help rain and snow drain away. The exterior has been protected over time with tar. And like all old buildings, it survived because generations of people considered it worth preserving.

Standing near the church made medieval Norway feel real. Unlike the huge stone cathedrals of continental Europe, this church reflected a society surrounded by forests and mountains. It was built from the material Norway had in abundance: wood.

## Vindhellavegen - The King's Road

A short distance from Borgund lies Vindhellavegen, one of the most famous sections of the old King's Road across Filefjell.

At first glance it looks like a stone path. Then you realise that people, horses, officials, traders and travellers used routes like this long before modern highways and tunnels existed.

Vindhellavegen climbs through the landscape using dramatic stone switchbacks. Walking there gave us a physical understanding of how difficult travel once was in Norway. Today we drive through mountains in tunnels, but for centuries people had to climb over passes, follow narrow roads and accept that weather controlled everything.

This stop created a powerful contrast: Borgund showed medieval spiritual life, while the King's Road showed the practical challenge of moving through a mountainous country.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Vindhellavegen stone switchbacks]

## The Lærdal Tunnel Incident

One of the funniest and most memorable route moments happened near Lærdal.

We wanted to drive the famous Snow Road, but the navigation system tried to send us through the Lærdal Tunnel. From a practical point of view, Google Maps was right. The tunnel is fast, direct and efficient.

But it was not what we wanted.

The Lærdal Tunnel is not just any tunnel. At 24.5 kilometres, it is the longest road tunnel in the world. It connects Lærdal and Aurland and allows drivers to avoid the mountain road, especially in winter. Inside the tunnel are specially lit caverns designed to reduce driver fatigue and break the monotony of such a long underground drive.

For commuters and winter travel, the tunnel is a masterpiece of engineering. For tourists searching for mountain views, it can be a trap.

We realised we had entered a long tunnel and had to correct the route. That small mistake became part of the story because it showed exactly what Norway is about: you often have a choice between the efficient engineered solution and the scenic mountain experience.

On this day, we wanted the mountain.

## Aurlandsfjellet - The Snow Road

Aurlandsfjellet, often called the Snow Road, became one of the highlights of the entire trip.

The road climbs high above the fjords and crosses a mountain plateau where snow can remain beside the road well into summer. In June, huge walls of snow still lined parts of the route. In some places they were several metres high.

The landscape felt almost Arctic. Lakes were partly frozen. Vegetation was sparse. The sky felt enormous. The road moved through a world of rock, snow, water and silence.

Unlike viewpoints where you stop for a few minutes, the Snow Road is an experience that unfolds slowly. The climb, the plateau, the snow walls, the sudden lakes and the descent toward the fjord all create a sense of journey.

This was one of the first moments when Norway felt unlike anywhere else we had travelled. It was not just beautiful. It was elemental.

## Stegastein Viewpoint

Eventually the Snow Road descends toward Aurlandsfjord. Before reaching the village below, we stopped at Stegastein.

Stegastein is one of Norway's most famous viewpoints. A wooden platform projects from the mountainside, hundreds of metres above the fjord. The end of the platform is fronted by glass, giving the feeling of standing in the air above the water.

The view explains immediately why Norway attracts visitors from around the world. Below lies Aurlandsfjord, a branch of the larger Sognefjord system. Small settlements sit along the water. Mountains rise steeply on both sides. Boats look tiny. Farms cling to slopes that seem almost impossible.

This was our first truly panoramic fjord view of the trip.

## Undredal

That evening we arrived in Undredal.

Undredal is a tiny fjord village with a population of roughly one hundred people. It is famous for goat cheese, its small stave church and its setting on the fjord.

After a day of mountain roads and dramatic viewpoints, Undredal felt peaceful. Small boats sat along the shore. The mountains reflected in the water. The long northern daylight continued into the evening.

One of the things we gradually learned throughout the trip was that Norway's beauty is not only found in the famous places. Sometimes it is found in villages where nothing much happens, and that is exactly the point.

Undredal felt like the kind of place where the landscape is the main event.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Undredal village and fjord]

## Why Norway Has So Many Tunnels

This day made us think about tunnels.

Norway has thousands of road tunnels because its geography is extremely difficult. Mountains, fjords, unstable slopes, snow and long winters make surface roads complicated. In many places, the most reliable way to connect communities is not to go around a mountain but through it.

The Lærdal Tunnel is the most famous example, but it is only one part of a much larger national pattern. Tunnels are not curiosities in Norway. They are basic infrastructure.

For us, the funny part was that one of the world's greatest tunnels became memorable because we were trying not to use it.

# Chapter 5 - Flåm Railway, Nærøyfjord and Voss

This chapter was the closest we came to the classic "Norway in a Nutshell" experience.

It included the Flåm Railway, a fjord cruise, mountain scenery, waterfalls and the feeling of moving through landscapes that are difficult to fully understand from a car window alone.

Before the trip, we had debated whether the Flåm Railway was necessary. After all, we were already driving through Norway. Would a train really add anything?

The answer was yes.

## Flåm

Flåm is a small village at the inner end of Aurlandsfjord. Despite its size, it is one of the most visited places in Norway because it sits at the intersection of several iconic travel experiences: fjord cruises, the Flåm Railway and dramatic mountain scenery.

The village itself is tourist-oriented, but its location is spectacular. Mountains rise steeply around the water, and trains, boats and buses all connect here.

For many travellers, Flåm is not only a destination but a gateway.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Flåm village and harbour]

## The Flåm Railway

The Flåm Railway is one of the steepest standard-gauge railway lines in the world.

In about 20 kilometres, it climbs from sea level at Flåm to the mountain station at Myrdal, nearly 900 metres above sea level. The route passes through tunnels, steep valleys, waterfalls and mountain farms.

What makes the railway special is not only the scenery but the engineering. Construction began in the early twentieth century and took many years. Much of the tunnel work was done manually. The railway had to solve an extreme challenge: how to connect the fjord with the mountain railway line above in a country of cliffs and waterfalls.

Before boarding, we asked a practical question: where should we sit?

For the journey starting in Flåm and climbing toward Myrdal, the left side is often recommended for many of the best views, though both sides have moments. The real advice is to stay alert, because the views change constantly.

The train was also a good lesson in why not every beautiful route can be experienced properly by car. Roads follow what is possible for cars. The railway follows a different historical and engineering logic, cutting through terrain in a way that feels completely different from driving.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Flåm Railway]

## Kjosfossen Waterfall

One of the most memorable moments on the Flåm Railway is the stop at Kjosfossen waterfall.

The train pauses so passengers can step out onto the platform and see the waterfall thundering down the mountainside. In summer, there is often a performance connected to the legend of the Huldra, a seductive forest spirit from Scandinavian folklore.

The combination of train, waterfall, mist and mythology makes the stop feel theatrical, but the power of the water is real. Kjosfossen is also connected to hydroelectric power, another recurring theme in Norway.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Kjosfossen waterfall]

## Why the Train Was Worth It

The Flåm Railway answered the question we had asked before the trip: is it worth taking the train if you are already driving through Norway?

Yes, because the train shows a different kind of landscape. It allowed us to stop thinking about navigation, parking, road choices and timing, and simply watch the landscape unfold.

It also created contrast. By this point we had driven scenic roads, entered long tunnels and crossed mountain areas. The train gave us a passenger's perspective through steep valleys and waterfalls. That difference made it worthwhile.

## Nærøyfjord Cruise

The fjord cruise was one of the most classic Norway experiences of the trip.

Nærøyfjord is a UNESCO-listed fjord and one of the narrowest fjords in Europe. It is a branch of the Sognefjord system and is known for steep mountains, waterfalls, tiny farms and dramatic cliffs rising directly from the water.

On a fjord cruise, the best place to be is outside whenever the weather allows it. The upper deck gives the widest views, but moving around the boat is important because the scenery changes on both sides.

From the boat, the scale becomes difficult to understand. Waterfalls drop from high cliffs. Remote farms appear on steep slopes where it seems impossible to live. Tiny villages sit at the edge of the water. Reflections appear in calmer sections of the fjord. As the fjord narrows, the mountains seem to close in.

One thing photographs struggle to capture is the vertical scale. From the boat, cliffs rise so high that the sky feels far away. Villages look impossibly small.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Nærøyfjord cruise]

## Are Fjords Fresh Water or Salt Water?

While travelling through the fjords, we asked whether fjord water is fresh or salty.

The answer is that most Norwegian fjords are salt water because they are connected to the sea. A fjord is essentially a deep glacial valley that has been flooded by the ocean.

However, rivers and waterfalls bring fresh water from mountains into the fjords. This can create a layer of less salty water near the surface, especially close to river mouths. Deeper down, the water is generally saltier.

This small question helped us understand what we were seeing. The fjords look like lakes in many places, but they are connected to the ocean.

## Gudvangen

Gudvangen sits at the end of Nærøyfjord and has long been a stopping point for travellers moving through the fjord region.

The village is surrounded by steep mountains and waterfalls, and it has developed as a tourism hub because of its location. Arriving by boat gives the place a dramatic sense of entrance: the fjord narrows, the mountains close in, and suddenly the village appears.

## Voss

After the fjord cruise and travel connections, we continued toward Voss.

Voss is often described as Norway's adventure capital. It is known for skiing, rafting, skydiving, hiking and mountain sports. For us, it was also a practical stop between the fjord region and Hardanger.

The town sits beside Vangsvatnet lake and is surrounded by mountains. It felt more active and sporty than the tiny fjord villages.

This was also part of understanding Norway: many towns are not famous for monuments but for access to outdoor activity.

# Chapter 6 - Hardanger: Waterfalls, Plateaus and Kinsarvik

After the Flåm and Voss area, the trip shifted toward Hardanger.

The Hardanger region felt different from the narrow fjords around Flåm. It was broader in places, with orchards, waterfalls, high plateaus and long drives along the water.

This part of the trip also introduced us to one of Norway's most impressive landscapes: Hardangervidda.

## Tvindefossen

Tvindefossen is a roadside waterfall near Voss.

It is not the tallest or most remote waterfall in Norway, but it is easy to access and visually beautiful, with water fanning down the rock in several streams.

One reason it became famous is a local legend that its water has rejuvenating powers. Whether or not anyone believes the legend, it adds charm to the stop.

For us, Tvindefossen was a reminder that in Norway even roadside stops can be impressive. A waterfall that might be a major attraction in another country can simply appear beside the road.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Tvindefossen]

## Hardangervidda

Hardangervidda is Europe's largest mountain plateau.

Driving across or near it feels very different from driving through valleys and fjords. The landscape becomes open, exposed and almost Arctic. Trees disappear. The weather feels harsher. Lakes, rocks, snow patches and wide skies dominate the view.

Hardangervidda is also an important habitat for wild reindeer. The plateau's climate and openness create a sense of wilderness that is rare in southern Europe.

Before going there, we wondered whether it was worth the drive. The answer depends on what a traveller wants. If someone expects villages, cafes and dramatic viewpoints every few minutes, Hardangervidda may feel empty. But if they want to experience Norway's high mountain plateau environment, it is absolutely worth seeing.

The emptiness is the point.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Hardangervidda plateau]

## Vøringsfossen

Vøringsfossen is one of Norway's most famous waterfalls.

The water drops from the Hardangervidda plateau into the Måbødalen valley, creating a dramatic vertical landscape. The main fall is often listed at around 182 metres total height.

What makes Vøringsfossen especially powerful is not only the waterfall itself but the surrounding canyon. Viewing platforms and paths allow visitors to see the falls from different angles. The combination of water, cliffs and depth creates one of the most dramatic stops in the Hardanger region.

This was another place where Norway's infrastructure impressed us. Instead of leaving visitors to unsafe informal viewpoints, the area has platforms, railings and paths designed to make the experience accessible while still dramatic.

## Eidfjord and the Hardangerfjord Area

Eidfjord sits at the end of a branch of Hardangerfjord and is often used as a base for visiting Vøringsfossen and Hardangervidda.

Even if we did not spend long in the town itself, the area helped connect several themes: fjords, mountain plateaus, waterfalls and hydroelectric power.

Hardanger is also known for fruit growing, especially apples, because the fjord climate creates milder conditions along the water than the high mountains above.

## Kinsarvik

Kinsarvik was part of our accommodation route in the Hardanger region.

The village sits where Sørfjorden and Eidfjorden meet the main Hardangerfjord. It is a practical and scenic base for exploring Hardanger, Ullensvang, waterfalls and routes toward Odda.

Staying in this area helped position us for the next major goal of the trip: Trolltunga.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Kinsarvik and Hardangerfjord]

## Why Norway Has So Much Hydroelectric Power

By this stage we had seen waterfalls, high mountains, reservoirs and power infrastructure. It made sense to ask why hydroelectricity is so important in Norway.

The answer is geography. Norway has mountains, heavy precipitation, snowmelt and steep drops from high plateaus to fjords and valleys. These conditions are ideal for hydropower.

Hydroelectricity helped Norway develop before oil wealth and remains central to the country's energy system. Later, when we saw power lines even in remote mountain areas, it made more sense. In Norway, dramatic landscapes are not only beautiful. They are also part of the country's energy infrastructure.

# Chapter 7 - Trolltunga: The Defining Hike

Trolltunga was the most demanding and probably the most unforgettable day of the trip.

Many places in Norway are beautiful from a viewpoint, a car window or a boat deck. Trolltunga is different. It requires effort. You do not simply arrive there. You earn it step by step.

Before the hike we had planned carefully. Parking matters. Timing matters. Weather matters. Fitness matters. Trolltunga is not a casual short walk to a viewpoint. It is a serious mountain hike.

## What Is Trolltunga?

Trolltunga means "The Troll's Tongue".

It is a rock ledge that projects horizontally from a mountain above Lake Ringedalsvatnet. The cliff is around 700 metres above the lake, and the shape of the rock makes it look like a stone tongue sticking out into empty space.

For years it was known mostly to locals and hikers. Social media transformed it into one of Norway's most famous natural landmarks.

But the photos can be misleading. They show the final rock, not the long hike required to reach it.

## Parking and the Start of the Hike

One of the most important practical decisions for Trolltunga is parking.

The main options are lower parking areas and the higher P3 parking at Mågelitopp. P3 is closer to the trail and saves a significant climb, but it must usually be booked in advance and has limited availability.

We had planned for P3, which made the day more manageable. Even with the higher start, the hike remained long and demanding.

This is one of the most important tips for future travellers: book Trolltunga parking early, especially in high season. Also check road access, weather and official safety advice before starting.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Trolltunga P3 parking and trail start]

## The Trail

The Trolltunga hike is usually described as around 27 kilometres round trip from the standard trailhead, with roughly 800 metres of elevation gain, depending on the starting point. From P3, the total distance and elevation are reduced, but it is still a long mountain hike.

The trail passes through different landscapes. At first there is climbing and rough terrain. Then the route opens into a high mountain environment with lakes, snow patches, rocks and wide views. The scenery is beautiful, but the distance is real.

This is one of the challenges of Trolltunga: the landscape is so impressive that it is easy to forget how far you still have to walk.

The hike requires patience. There is no single dramatic reveal at the beginning. Instead, the day builds slowly: climb, plateau, lake, snow, rocks, more walking, more views, and finally the famous ledge.

The trail is not only a physical route. It is also a mental one. On the way out, excitement keeps pulling you forward. Every ridge might be the last one. Every view feels like it could be the main view. But Trolltunga makes you wait.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Trolltunga trail, lakes and plateau]

## The Rock

Arriving at Trolltunga is emotional because the place is so recognisable.

After hours of walking, the rock suddenly becomes real. It is no longer an image from the internet. It is there, hanging above the lake, with mountains stretching around it.

The ledge is dramatic, but the surrounding landscape is just as important. The lake far below, the cliffs, the open sky and the sense of height all combine to create the experience.

Standing there, it is easy to understand why so many people want the photo. But the memory is not only the photo. It is the entire day that led to it: the early start, the preparation, the walking, the snow patches, the tired legs, the repeated question of how much farther it could be, and then finally the view.

That is why Trolltunga felt different from many other famous viewpoints. We did not simply arrive, take a photo and leave. We reached it after investing a full day of effort.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Lital and Gilad at Trolltunga]

## How Was Trolltunga Formed?

Trolltunga was shaped by geological processes connected to glaciation and erosion.

During the Ice Age, glaciers carved the valleys and mountains of Norway. As the ice moved and later retreated, it left behind steep cliffs, exposed rock and unusual formations. Trolltunga survived as a projecting piece of rock while the surrounding landscape was carved away.

The result is one of the most photogenic natural formations in Norway.

## Who Built the Trail?

After the hike, we asked how a trail like this is built and maintained.

The original route was not a modern tourist path. Over time, as Trolltunga became more famous, the trail had to be improved for safety and erosion control.

In many Norwegian mountain areas, skilled stone workers, including Sherpa teams from Nepal, have helped build stone steps and reinforce trails. This kind of work is slow, physical and highly skilled. It protects the mountain from erosion while making the route safer for hikers.

## How Did They Bring Equipment?

Another question was how materials and equipment reach such remote places.

In many mountain projects, heavy materials are transported by helicopter. Some work still requires manual carrying and local placement by hand. Trail building in this terrain is not simple construction. It is a combination of engineering, physical labour and respect for the landscape.

## Why Are There Power Lines in Remote Places?

We also noticed power infrastructure in places that seemed remote.

This connects to Norway's hydroelectric system. Mountains, lakes, reservoirs and waterfalls are part of the country's energy network. Power lines may cross areas that look wild because they connect production sites, communities and the national grid.

This was one of the recurring lessons of the trip: Norway's dramatic landscapes are both natural and functional. They are beautiful, but they also provide energy, transport routes and economic value.

## The Return

The return from Trolltunga is mentally different from the way out.

On the way there, excitement pulls you forward. On the way back, the distance becomes more obvious. The same beautiful plateau feels longer. Feet get tired. Small climbs feel bigger.

This is why Trolltunga should be treated with respect. The famous viewpoint is only halfway through the day.

Still, the tiredness became part of the achievement. By the time we finished, we knew this would be one of the defining memories of the trip.

## What Trolltunga Taught Us

Trolltunga taught us that Norway's most famous views are not always easy.

Some places can be reached by car, ferry or train. Others require effort. The difficulty is part of the value. The long walk makes the final view more meaningful.

It was demanding, beautiful and unforgettable.

# Chapter 8 - Odda, Rosendal and the Ferry Roads

After Trolltunga, we needed a slower day.

The previous day had been physically intense, so the next stage of the trip was about recovery, fjord driving, villages and coastal life. This part of Norway felt softer than the high mountain plateau: more water, more orchards, more ferries, more small settlements.

## Odda

Odda is one of the main bases for Trolltunga and sits at the end of Sørfjorden, a branch of Hardangerfjord.

Historically, Odda was an industrial town, connected to hydropower and heavy industry. Today it is strongly associated with outdoor tourism, especially Trolltunga.

For us, Odda was practical and symbolic. It was the place connected to the hardest hike of the trip, but also a reminder that Norwegian fjord towns are not only postcard villages. They are working towns with industry, roads, services and real communities.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Odda]

## Driving the Hardangerfjord Area

The drive after Odda followed fjords, small settlements and mountain edges.

This kind of driving is one of the pleasures of Norway. The road itself becomes part of the sightseeing. Sometimes the fjord is beside you. Sometimes the road enters a tunnel. Sometimes a ferry becomes the continuation of the route.

At this point in the trip, we had learned not to think of ferries as interruptions. In Norway, ferries are often simply part of the road.

## Rosendal

Rosendal is one of the most charming villages in the Hardangerfjord region.

It sits between fjord and mountains and feels calmer than the heavily visited Flåm area. Rosendal is known especially for Baroniet Rosendal, often described as Norway's only barony.

## Baroniet Rosendal

Baroniet Rosendal dates from the seventeenth century and is a small manor estate surrounded by gardens and dramatic scenery.

What makes it interesting is the contrast. After days of mountains, waterfalls and fjords, Rosendal introduced a different kind of history: aristocratic, domestic and cultivated. The manor, gardens and surrounding landscape show another side of Norway's past.

For travellers, Rosendal is a good reminder that fjord Norway is not only about viewpoints. It also has villages, estates, gardens and cultural history.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Rosendal and Baroniet Rosendal]

## Ferry Culture

By this stage of the trip, ferries had become part of the experience.

In many countries, taking a ferry feels like a special event. In Norway, ferries often function as floating road segments. You drive on, cross the fjord, drive off and continue.

This makes sense because fjords cut deeply into the land. Without ferries, many routes would require long detours around water. Bridges and tunnels exist in many places, but ferries remain essential.

Some ferries are electric or hybrid, reflecting Norway's investment in cleaner transport. Payment is often automatic through number plate recognition or transport systems, which can surprise visitors expecting a ticket booth.

For us, ferries became part of the rhythm of travelling in Norway. They created pauses: a few minutes to get out of the car, look at the water, watch the mountains and reset before the next drive.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: ferry crossing]

## Are Ferries Roads?

In Norway, the answer is almost yes.

Ferries are not side attractions. They are often integrated into the road network. Navigation apps treat them as part of the route. Locals use them for normal travel. Tourists use them for scenery.

This changed how we understood Norwegian geography. The country did not try to eliminate every obstacle. Instead, it built a transport system that combines roads, tunnels, bridges and ferries.

# Chapter 9 - Kvam, Steinsdalsfossen and the Road to Bergen

The next stage took us from the Hardanger region toward Bergen.

By now we had seen many waterfalls, but Steinsdalsfossen still stood out because it offers something unusual: the chance to walk behind the waterfall without getting completely wet.

## Kvam and Norheimsund

Kvam is a municipality in Vestland county, located along the Hardangerfjord. Norheimsund is one of its main centres and is often used as a stop between Hardanger and Bergen.

The area combines fjord views, small-town life and access to one of Norway's most visitor-friendly waterfalls.

For us, this was a transition area: no longer the intense mountain world of Trolltunga, not yet the urban life of Bergen.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Kvam and Norheimsund]

## Steinsdalsfossen

Steinsdalsfossen is one of Norway's most popular waterfalls, not because it is the tallest, but because visitors can walk behind it.

A path climbs from the parking area and passes behind the falling water, creating the rare experience of standing between the rock and the waterfall. You hear the roar of water, feel the spray and look outward through a moving curtain.

We had already seen powerful waterfalls, but Steinsdalsfossen felt playful and accessible. It is the kind of place that works for almost every traveller: families, road trippers, photographers and people who want a short but memorable stop.

During the trip we also discussed the story often told about the waterfall's unusual path. Whether described as planning, discovery or a happy accident, the result is the same: Steinsdalsfossen became famous because people can safely experience the waterfall from behind.

That idea stayed with us because it matched a recurring feeling from the trip. Some of Norway's best moments were not carefully staged. They happened because a road, a path, a ferry or a viewpoint allowed people to get close to the landscape in a simple and direct way.

## The Drive to Bergen

The drive toward Bergen felt like the closing movement of the road trip.

After days of mountains, fjords, ferries and small villages, we were approaching Norway's second-largest city. The landscape remained beautiful, but the traffic and urban feeling gradually increased.

Returning the rental car marked a shift. Until now, the car had given us freedom: detours, mountain roads, late evening stops, waterfalls and villages. In Bergen, the trip would become more walkable and urban again.

# Chapter 10 - Bergen: The City Between Mountains and Sea

Bergen was the natural ending point for the trip.

If Oslo introduced us to modern Norway and the fjords showed us Norway's geography, Bergen connected both to history, trade and coastal culture.

The city sits between mountains and sea. It rains often, but that is part of its identity. Wooden houses climb hillsides. Ferries and boats move through the harbour. Mountains rise directly behind the city.

Bergen feels like a city shaped by weather, trade and geography.

## First Feeling in Bergen

After so many days of driving, Bergen felt different immediately. We were back in a city, but it was not a flat city or a city that hides nature at its edges. Bergen is squeezed between water and mountains. Wherever you walk, the landscape is present.

The harbour, the old wooden buildings, the steep streets, the mountains and the changing weather all create a very specific atmosphere. Bergen feels historic, but also alive. It is not a museum city. It is a working coastal city that still carries the memory of trade, fish, ships and rain.

## Bryggen

Bryggen is Bergen's most famous historic district and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The colourful wooden buildings along the harbour were once the centre of Hanseatic trade in Bergen. German merchants of the Hanseatic League controlled much of the dried fish trade from northern Norway to Europe for centuries. Dried cod from the north was exchanged for grain and goods from continental Europe, making Bergen one of the most important trading cities in Scandinavia.

Walking through Bryggen is like entering a narrow wooden maze. The front facades are famous, but the most interesting parts are often the passages between and behind the buildings: uneven floors, wooden walls, small shops and the feeling of an old trading world preserved inside a modern city.

Bryggen also tells a story of survival. Bergen suffered several major fires over the centuries, and wooden buildings are always vulnerable. The fact that Bryggen still exists, even after damage, rebuilding and preservation, makes it feel fragile and precious.

For us, Bryggen was important because it showed that Norway's wealth and identity were shaped not only by oil and modern industry, but also by centuries of maritime trade.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Bryggen facades and wooden passages]

## Bergen Fish Market

The Bergen Fish Market is one of the city's best-known public spaces.

It reflects Bergen's connection to the sea and to seafood. For visitors, it can feel touristy, but it is still worth seeing because it sits at the heart of the harbour area and connects modern Bergen to its maritime identity.

Norway is one of the world's major seafood countries, especially known today for salmon. In Bergen, fish is not just food. It is part of the city's story.

The market also helped connect what we saw in Bergen to the older history of Bryggen. The city grew because of trade from the sea, and even today the harbour remains the centre of the visitor experience.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Bergen Fish Market]

## Fløibanen and Mount Fløyen

One of Bergen's classic experiences is taking the Fløibanen funicular up to Mount Fløyen.

The ride is short, but it gives one of the best accessible views over the city. From above, Bergen's geography becomes clear: harbour, old town, islands, water and mountains all packed closely together.

Mount Fløyen is also a gateway to walking trails, making it another example of the Norwegian city-nature connection. Just as Oslo has Nordmarka connected by public transport, Bergen has mountains that begin almost inside the city.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: view from Mount Fløyen]

## Ulriken

Ulriken is the highest of Bergen's seven mountains.

We discussed whether it was possible to reach the highest point by car. The answer is that the summit itself is not reached by ordinary private car, but visitors can use the Ulriken cable car or hike up depending on weather, time and fitness.

Ulriken offers one of the best panoramic views of Bergen and the surrounding fjords, islands and mountains. Compared with Fløyen, it feels higher and more dramatic.

For travellers with limited time, Ulriken is one of the best ways to understand Bergen's setting. The city is not simply beside mountains. It is surrounded by them.

What made Ulriken interesting for us was the same thing that made Holmenkollen interesting in Oslo: the combination of city and mountain. In many places, you choose between urban travel and nature travel. In Bergen, the two are connected.

[PHOTO PLACEHOLDER: Ulriken viewpoint]

## Løvstakken

Løvstakken is another of Bergen's seven mountains and is popular with locals.

We considered it as a less touristy hiking option. Compared with Ulriken, it is more of a local favourite and less of a classic visitor attraction. It offers excellent views over Bergen without the same tourist infrastructure.

This distinction is useful for future travellers: choose Ulriken for the iconic high viewpoint and easier cable car access; choose Løvstakken if you want a more local hiking experience.

## Bergen Reflection

Bergen was a fitting final city because it gathered many themes of the trip.

Water was everywhere. Mountains were close. History was visible. The harbour connected Norway to the wider world. Outdoor life remained part of the city.

After the long drive from Oslo, the fjords, the train, the cruise, the waterfalls and Trolltunga, Bergen felt like the natural end of the journey: a city built at the meeting point of sea, mountains and trade.

# Epilogue - Why This Trip Stayed With Us

Norway is beautiful in an obvious way: fjords, waterfalls, mountains, snow roads and viewpoints.

But the deeper memory of the trip is not only the scenery. It is the way the country works with its geography.

Where there are mountains, Norway builds tunnels.

Where there are fjords, Norway uses ferries.

Where there are waterfalls, Norway generates power.

Where there is snow, Norway builds a culture around skiing.

Where there are forests, Norway makes them accessible from the city.

The trip from Oslo to Bergen taught us that Norway's beauty is not separate from daily life. It shapes the roads, the towns, the sports, the energy system, the architecture and the national identity.

For us, the most meaningful memories were not only the famous places. They were also the questions we asked along the way.

Why are fjords salty?

Why is Norway so rich?

Why are there so many tunnels?

Why do people ski so much?

How do you build a trail to Trolltunga?

Why does a ferry feel like a road?

Why are all the people in Vigeland Park naked?

Those questions turned the trip into more than a route. They turned it into a story.

And that is what this journey became: not just a visit to Norway, but an attempt to understand it.
